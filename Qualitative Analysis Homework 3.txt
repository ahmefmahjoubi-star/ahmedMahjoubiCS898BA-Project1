When analyzing the qualitative results of my pipeline, I noticed some clear trends in how the model learned. During my initial baseline run, the network heavily overfit the training set. It memorized the data incredibly fast, dropping the training loss from about 1.399 down to nearly zero and hitting 100% training accuracy by the 10th epoch. However, my validation loss hit its absolute lowest point of 0.8731 at Epoch 3 and then rapidly degraded, climbing all the way up to 1.6464, while the validation accuracy plateaued around 73%. Because my architecture uses a large linear layer with over 32,000 incoming features, this massive gap confirmed that the network was simply memorizing my 711 training images rather than learning generalizable patterns.

To combat this and stabilize the training dynamics, I relied on my data preprocessing and augmentation pipeline. By properly partitioning my 1,016 total images and applying augmentation techniques like random flips, rotations, and scaling, I was able to artificially increase the input variance. This forced the convolutional layers to stop fixating on static background noise and instead learn the actual morphological features of the six different fish classes. As a result, the early-epoch gradient updates became much smoother and less erratic.

To further rein in the overfitting, I set up a 12-combination grid search to systematically tune my hyperparameters. I quickly found that a learning rate of 0.01 caused severe gradient overshooting, pushing validation losses incredibly high, while 0.0001 converged far too slowly for a 10-epoch window. Settling on a learning rate of 0.001 gave me the perfect step size for steady, accelerated convergence. Additionally, increasing my batch size to 64 helped smooth out the stochastic gradient noise, yielding much cleaner parameter updates.

Ultimately, the most significant improvement came from applying an L2 weight decay of 0.001. This effectively penalized the large weight magnitudes accumulating in my dense layers, directly suppressing the network's capacity to overfit. My final, optimal configuration—a batch size of 64, a learning rate of 0.001, and an L2 penalty of 0.001—successfully dropped my lowest validation loss down to 1.4633, proving to be the strongest defense against memorization while keeping the training stable.
